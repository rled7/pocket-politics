How a bill becomes law

Every step a bill takes — what must happen, what can happen, and where bills die (most do). Federal process; states mirror it closely.
How amendments happen

An amendment changes a bill's text. There are three main moments:

  • In committee (markup): members offer amendments and vote them up or down before the bill is reported out. Most changes happen here.
  • On the floor: members propose amendments during debate. The House limits them (often a "closed" or "structured" rule set by the Rules Committee, and amendments must be germane — on-topic). The Senate usually allows non-germane amendments — this is how unrelated provisions ("riders") get attached.
  • Reconciling the two chambers: if the House and Senate pass different versions, a conference committee (or amendment exchange) merges them — itself a round of amending.

A constitutional amendment is entirely different and far rarer: 2/3 of both chambers (or a convention) propose it, then 3/4 of the states (38) must ratify it.

This is the standard federal path; exact procedure varies (e.g., budget reconciliation bypasses the Senate filibuster). For the official version see Congress.gov — The Legislative Process ↗.
